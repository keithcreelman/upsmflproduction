1. League Overview and Governance

UPS is a 12-team salary-cap dynasty league built around long-term roster construction, aggressive trading, contract strategy, and owner participation. This section answers the basic question: what kind of league this is, who belongs in it, and who decides when the book is not yet perfect.

Settled core
12 teams 4 divisions Salary-cap dynasty Commissioner authority in silent areas New-owner relevant

League identity

  • The league has 12 teams divided into 4 divisions.
  • Divisions are realigned every 3 years based on historical all-play results.
  • The league runs both the UPS Championship bracket and the Hawktuah Bowl.
  • The entire format is designed to reward long-term planning, contract skill, and active owner involvement rather than passive roster maintenance.

Owner expectations

  • Owners are expected to stay active on Discord year-round.
  • Owners are expected to respond to trade activity in a timely way.
  • The Rookie Draft and Free Agent Auction are mandatory league events.
  • Owners must compete in good faith for the full fantasy season, even when out of title contention.
  • Collusion or competitive-balance manipulation is grounds for dismissal.

Membership and probation

  • New owners go through an admission process and league-knowledge check.
  • New owners serve a two-season probation period.
  • The league may remove a probationary owner early for repeated penalties, lack of progress, or behavior that threatens league balance.
  • When a new owner enters under the right conditions, the league may use a dispersal draft rather than handing over one roster untouched.

Commissioner authority

  • The commissioner decides issues not fully resolved by the written rules.
  • League precedent matters and should guide future decisions where possible.
  • The commissioner may consult senior members or the full league when a ruling affects league-wide balance.
  • The working standard is always the best interest of the league, not convenience for one franchise.

Example: new owner via dispersal

A new owner joins and two existing owners choose to throw their players and eligible picks into a shared pool. The commissioner randomizes draft order, runs the dispersal in Discord, and once an owner commits to that pool they cannot back out later just because the room turns against them. That is not a normal trade or cut process. It is a league-structure remedy for owner transition.

2. League Calendar and Event Windows

UPS runs on a yearly rhythm. Owners need to understand which deadlines are hard, which are soft operational targets, and which events can move a little when real life forces the commissioner to shift the league calendar.

Working draft
March rollover target Memorial Day rookie draft Last-weekend-of-July auction target Timing can flex for logistics
  1. January-February: offseason. League season is complete, but teams are already planning for rollover.
  2. March: new league year begins. Contracts roll forward. The target is March 1, but the commissioner may extend the launch window to roughly March 15 when needed.
  3. April 30: last day for expiring rookie extensions.
  4. First week of May: expired rookie auction.
  5. Memorial Day weekend: rookie draft, live and mandatory.
  6. Late July: free agent auction. The normal target is the last weekend of July, but actual timing may flex for availability and commissioner logistics.
  7. Within 48 hours before auction start: final cut-down day normally lands here so cap hits can be finalized before bidding begins.
  8. After auction completion: waivers open under the league's in-season acquisition pattern.
  9. Last Sunday before Week 1: contract finalization deadline.
  10. Thanksgiving kickoff: trade deadline.
  11. Week 15: fantasy playoffs begin and run three weeks.

Operating principle

The auction's normal target period is stable. The exact date is not. If the commissioner moves the auction to fit league availability, that is an operational calendar adjustment, not a rule violation by itself.

Example: flexible auction timing

If the last weekend of July is impossible because multiple owners are traveling, the commissioner can move the auction into early August. The rule is still being followed if the league is given a clear event date, the cut-down window is preserved, and the rest of the workflow is adjusted around the new operational schedule.

3. Rosters, Taxi, IR, and Lineups

This section governs how many players you can carry, what belongs on active roster versus taxi versus IR, when lineup locks happen, and why UPS treats roster discipline as a real part of competitive integrity.

Settled core
Minimum active roster 27 Auction max 35 Post-deadline max 30 Taxi max 10 Rosters module heavy

Roster counts

  • Minimum active roster during the fantasy season: 27 players.
  • Maximum roster during the free agent auction: 35 players.
  • Maximum roster after the contract deadline date: 30 players.
  • IR players do not count toward the active roster limit.

Starting lineup

  • 1 QB
  • 2 RB
  • 2 WR
  • 1 TE
  • 2 Flex (RB/WR/TE)
  • 1 SuperFlex (QB/RB/WR/TE)
  • 1 K
  • 1 P
  • 2 DL, 2 LB, 2 DB, 1 D-Flex

Lineup lock

Players lock at the kickoff of their own NFL game. Once a player's game starts, that player cannot be subbed in or out.

IR relief

Eligible IR states receive 50% automatic cap relief. Supported categories include IR, COVID-era IR, holdouts, and certain suspension paths.

3-starting-QB limit

After the contract deadline date, teams may carry only three starting quarterbacks, subject to the stated taxi exception.

Taxi squad rules

  • Taxi squad maximum is 10 players.
  • The taxi group must contain at least 1 IDP.
  • Only players drafted in the UPS rookie draft in Round 2 or later are taxi-eligible.
  • Taxi eligibility lasts for the player's first three NFL years.
  • Eligible rookies must be demoted to taxi by the contract deadline date if that is where they belong.

Taxi on trade

  • If a traded player is taxi-valid for the new team, UPS should return that player to taxi automatically.
  • If the trade lands right before kickoff and the demotion cannot reasonably happen in time, leniency can carry the demotion to the following week.
  • Taxi players do not count toward trade-salary calculations.
Examples: roster and lineup rules

Taxi example: You draft a running back at 2.04. He is taxi-eligible because he is Round 2+. You may keep him on taxi during his first three NFL seasons if he remains otherwise eligible.

Kickoff example: If a WR's NFL game started at 1:00 PM ET, you cannot swap him out at 1:07 PM ET after he goose-eggs his first two drives.

QB rule example: If you enter the season with three starters and then a taxi QB becomes a starter after Week 1, that taxi-only exception does not automatically create a drop obligation if the rule's exception applies. If a fourth starting QB appears and does count under the rule, you must get compliant by the following Thursday kickoff.

5. Rookie Draft

The rookie draft is the most important annual league event. It creates rookie contracts, drives draft-pick value, feeds the taxi squad, and shapes cap strategy for multiple seasons.

Settled core
Mandatory event Memorial Day weekend 6 rounds Round 1 option class starts in 2025

Draft format

  • The draft is live and mandatory, typically on the Sunday of Memorial Day weekend.
  • Round 1 rookies must remain on the active roster.
  • Rounds 2-6 are taxi-eligible.
  • Round 6 picks cannot be traded and must be used on an IDP, kicker, or punter.

Draft order

  • Rounds 1-5 follow the prior season's playoff finish logic.
  • Round 6 uses a random order.
  • All drafted rookies receive a 3-year rookie contract.

Example: first-round rookie salary

If you draft a player at 1.03, the rookie salary is $13K per year on a 3-year rookie contract. That player stays on your active roster, not taxi.

Example: second-round rookie on taxi

If you draft a linebacker at 2.08, the rookie salary is $5K, the player is taxi-eligible, and you can keep him there so long as he remains within the league's taxi eligibility window.

First-round rookie option

Starting with the 2025 draft class, first-round picks receive an option-year path on top of the base rookie deal. The option must be exercised before the contract deadline of the final rookie-contract season.

Current working offensive example: 15K / 15K / 15K / option 20K, followed by any later extension path.

6. Expired Rookie Auction

Expired rookies that do not get extended do not simply vanish into generic free agency. They go through their own process first.

Settled core
First week of May 2-3 day nomination window Starting bid = prior salary + $1K

Who enters

Any player with an expired rookie contract who is not extended by the deadline enters this auction.

How bidding works

Bidding mirrors the free agent auction. The high bidder must remain highest for 36 hours.

Contract result

Winners may assign 1-3 years. If the contract is longer than 1 year, it must be finalized by the September contract deadline.

Example: expired rookie auction bid

A running back just finished a rookie contract at $6K. He was not extended by the deadline. He enters the expired rookie auction with a minimum opening bid of $7K. If you win him and want a 3-year deal, that contract still must be filed by the September deadline.

7. Free Agent Auction

The free agent auction is the league's largest roster-building event outside the rookie draft. It is mandatory, time-sensitive, and heavily tied to cap management, nomination discipline, and roster compliance.

Working draft
Target start: last weekend of July Proxy bidding 36-hour standing bid Cap floor $260K

Auction basics

  • The auction usually starts on the last weekend of July.
  • The actual date may shift for league availability and commissioner logistics.
  • The auction is a mandatory league event.
  • The auction uses an eBay-style proxy bidding model.
  • A player is won when the high bid stands for 36 hours.

Auction roster and cap standards

  • Maximum roster size during the auction is 35.
  • Minimum roster size by auction completion is 27.
  • You must be able to field a valid starting lineup by auction completion.
  • The salary cap floor is $260K, due by auction completion or the contract deadline.
  • Once the floor is reached, later trade or IR changes can take you below it without retroactive violation.

Nomination duty

  • The current working rule is 2 nominations per cycle.
  • The first cycle opens at 12:00 PM ET on auction day and runs to midnight.
  • Subsequent cycles reset at 12:00 AM ET.
  • Missed nominations incur escalating penalties starting at $3K.
  • The exact escalation ladder still needs final cleanup and codification.

Stopping nomination duty

  • A franchise may stop nominating once it is nomination-complete.
  • That means it already has, or is currently leading enough players to finish with, the minimum roster and a valid lineup.
  • If that leading bid is lost later, a supplemental nomination may reopen.
  • Stopping because you are complete is not the same as quitting. It is a governed stop point.

Pre-auction cut-down day

The league normally wants the final cut-down day to land within 48 hours before auction start so cap hits and roster accounting can settle before bidding starts. That window is a normal operating target, not an eternal fixed number.

Examples: auction behavior

Nomination-complete example: You need only one punter to finish a valid lineup and you are already the high bidder on a punter. UPS can treat you as nomination-complete. If you lose that punter later, nomination duty may reopen.

Spending example: If the dashboard says the maximum you can spend to fill every remaining roster spot is $20K, but you only need the minimum roster and lineup completion, your true flexible spend may be higher. It is still your job not to overbid into an impossible roster shape.

Cap floor example: A team at $247K after the auction cannot simply shrug and say it will fix things later. It must meet the floor by auction completion or by the contract deadline through real roster or contract action.

8. Waivers, FCFS, Adds, and Drops

Once the auction is over, the league shifts into its in-season acquisition rhythm. Owners need to know when a player belongs in blind bid, when FCFS is open, and when a release is blocked by kickoff or auction timing.

Settled core
Blind bid Thu/Fri/Sat/Sun at 9 AM FCFS after Sunday waiver run FCFS salary $1K Rosters action launcher

Blind Bid Waivers

  • Blind-bid waivers run on Thursday, Friday, Saturday, and Sunday at 9:00 AM during the NFL season.
  • The winning blind-bid amount becomes the player's current-season salary.
  • Ties break by all-play, overall record, total points, then head-to-head.
  • Before and through Week 1, the league uses a custom preseason order based on prior season finishing context.

FCFS and direct adds

  • After the Sunday morning waiver run, players move into first-come, first-served acquisition.
  • FCFS stays open until the player's own NFL kickoff.
  • Any FCFS add defaults to $1K and a 1-year contract unless another rule later changes it.
  • On the site, owners should see one governed acquisition path rather than separate confusing buttons.

Drop timing

  • You may not drop a player after that player's NFL game has kicked off.
  • Drops are blocked during the pre-auction roster lock window.
  • Drops are also blocked while the free agent auction is active.
  • Outside those windows, drop remains a core roster-management tool.

Owner responsibility

  • The system does not excuse you from cap or roster mistakes.
  • You are responsible for having the roster space and cap room to make a legal acquisition.
  • UPS should show the right action path, but it should not silently legalize a bad move.
Examples: acquisition timing

Waiver bid example: On Thursday, you want a backup RB who is on no roster. If waivers are the active mode, the correct action is a blind bid, not an immediate add.

Sunday FCFS example: After the Sunday morning run, a free-agent linebacker is now available by FCFS until his game kicks off. If you add him, his current-season salary is $1K.

Drop example: If your kicker's game starts at 4:25 PM ET, you cannot cut him at 4:40 PM ET because he already kicked off.

9. Contracts and Cap Management

This is the strategic center of the league. Contracts determine salary, guarantee exposure, cap penalties, extension pricing, rookie control, auction flexibility, and how teams carry risk. This section is intentionally detailed and example-heavy.

Working draft
Max 3 years on any contract Max 6 non-rookie 3-year deals Max 5 loaded contracts 75% guarantee framework Contract Actions module

Contract glossary

  • Salary: the player's current-year salary.
  • Years Remaining: seasons left on the deal.
  • TCV: total contract value across the remaining deal.
  • AAV: average annual value, used as the baseline for extension pricing.
  • Guarantee: the portion of the contract protected against an early cut.
  • Cap Penalty: dead money created when guaranteed value remains after a release.

Universal contract limits

  • No player may have more than 3 years on a contract at one time.
  • No more than 6 non-rookie 3-year contracts may be carried from contract deadline through season end.
  • No more than 5 loaded contracts may be carried at one time.
  • The first year of any multi-year deal must carry at least 20% of total contract value.

Contract types

  • Rookie: used for rookie-year players. Drafted rookies receive 3 years by default.
  • Veteran: the normal contract type for non-rookie acquisitions.
  • WW: default deal for waiver or FCFS additions, usually 1 year until changed by later action.
  • Loaded: veteran contracts that are front-loaded or back-loaded rather than evenly split.

Loaded contracts

  • Front-loaded: Year 1 salary is above AAV.
  • Back-loaded: Year 1 salary is below AAV.
  • The contract still has to total correctly across all years.
  • The current-season salary of an in-season acquisition may not be changed just because you want a prettier long-term shape.

Rookie contracts and rookie status

  • Drafted rookies receive 3 years automatically.
  • Rookie contracts are evenly distributed and cannot be restructured while the player is still under rookie control.
  • Rookies acquired in-season usually begin as WW/default acquisitions and can later move into rookie-appropriate handling through MYM or extension paths.
  • Taxi-eligible players carry no guarantee until activated.

First-round rookie option

  • Applies to first-round rookie picks beginning with the 2025 draft class.
  • The option must be exercised before the contract deadline of the player's final rookie season.
  • Current working offensive model adds a fifth controlled year at a higher salary band.
  • The defensive pricing formula still needs final league confirmation.

Multi-year auction contracts

Players won in the free agent auction, expired rookie auction, or pre-deadline waiver window may be given 2- or 3-year auction contracts. If you do not file a multi-year version by the deadline, the default result is a 1-year contract.

See auction contract examples

Mid-Year Multi (MYM)

  • Each team gets a maximum of 3 MYM actions per season.
  • MYM converts a 1-year deal into a multi-year contract at the same salary.
  • MYM cannot be loaded.
  • Preseason acquisitions not contracted by the deadline can usually be MYM'd by the end of NFL Week 2.
  • In-season waiver acquisitions can usually be MYM'd within 2 weeks of acquisition.

Extensions

  • Final-year players are generally extension-eligible through the contract deadline date.
  • Preseason waiver and rookie-edge cases may create a Week 4 extension path.
  • Players acquired via trade in the final year of their deal usually must be extended within 4 weeks.
  • Extensions add 1 or 2 years and reset TCV and guarantees.
Extension schedule 1 year 2 years Applies to
Schedule 1 +$10K AAV +$20K AAV QB / RB / WR / TE
Schedule 2 +$3K AAV +$5K AAV DB / LB / DL / K / P

Example: Schedule 1 extension

A wide receiver has 1 year left at $17K AAV. You extend him for 1 additional year. His extension schedule adds $10K to the new extension-year AAV, creating a fresh contract structure built from the new TCV.

Example: Schedule 2 extension

A linebacker has 1 year left at $10K. You extend him for 2 years. The extension adds $5K to the AAV basis used for those new years. The resulting deal is materially cheaper than a Schedule 1 offensive extension.

Restructures

Restructures clearly exist in league practice and on the site, but the clean final written rule still needs tightening. The strongest working points are:

  • Restructures are not for rookie contracts.
  • Restructures are generally preseason or pre-deadline tools, not a free-for-all at any time.
  • Mid-season acquisitions should not quietly unlock restructure abuse.
  • The yearly restructure cap and exact extension interaction still need final cleanup.

Tags

Tags belong in the league's operational rule system and current site design, but the clean written rule has drifted. This section should eventually codify:

  • tag types and eligibility
  • tag salary or pricing formula
  • tag timing window
  • renewal or repeat-tag limits
  • tag interaction with extensions, trades, and auction paths

Guarantees and earned value

  • Most contracts carry a 75% TCV guarantee.
  • Earn schedule: 0% before October, then 25% on October 1, 50% on November 1, and 75% on December 1.
  • At the next season rollover, the prior season salary is fully earned.
  • Veteran or WW contracts under $5K with one original year may be cap-free cuts.
  • WW contracts at $5K+ use a 65% earned / 35% penalty style exception.

Cap penalty rules

  • Base formula: (TCV x 75%) - salary earned.
  • Cap penalties after the auction usually apply to the following season.
  • Cap penalties before the roster-lock period hit the current season.
  • Taxi-eligible players cut before activation and before the relevant deadline may be cap-free.
  • Retired and legal-removal edge cases can qualify for cap-free treatment under special league rules.
Examples: contracts and cap penalties

Loaded contract example: A 3-year $90K deal can be front-loaded as 40 / 30 / 20. It can be back-loaded only if Year 1 still carries at least 20% of the TCV.

MYM example: You add a player preseason at $4K and do not file an auction contract. Before the Week 2 deadline, you use MYM and turn the deal into a multi-year contract at the same $4K salary basis.

Cap penalty example: A contract with a $100K TCV has a $75K guarantee. If you cut the player before October with nothing earned, the penalty is $75K. If the player is cut after October earnings begin, earned value reduces the dead money.

Taxi cut example: A taxi-eligible player who has never been activated and is cut before the relevant deadline can be released without a cap penalty.

10. Trades and Salary Movement

Trades are live all offseason and in-season until Thanksgiving kickoff. The league wants active trading, but not chaos: asset eligibility, salary movement, response timing, kickoff locks, and taxi handling all need to be understood.

Working draft
Trade window starts with new league year Trade deadline = Thanksgiving kickoff Two-team self-serve release 1 Trades workspace

Trade window

  • Trading opens with the start of the new league year.
  • Trading remains open through the offseason, during the auction, and into the season.
  • The trade deadline is the kickoff of the NFL Thanksgiving Day game.
  • After that kickoff, no new trades may be launched for that league year window.

Eligible assets

  • Players with 1 or more years remaining may be traded.
  • Expired rookies may be traded only up to the rookie-extension deadline.
  • Other expired contracts are not trade-eligible.
  • Current-year picks and picks 1 year into the future are eligible.
  • Round 6 picks are not trade-eligible.

Trade salary

  • Salary may be included in trades.
  • The governing base is the current-season salary of the outgoing selected active-roster or IR players.
  • Taxi salaries are excluded from the formula.
  • The maximum extra salary a side may send is 50% of the summed eligible outgoing salary.
  • Salary movement posts mirrored cap adjustments between the two sides.

Kickoff lock and same-week handling

  • If any included player's game has already started, the trade may not process for the current scoring week.
  • Same-week publish is a hard block once kickoff has happened.
  • The current working model is deferred processing at the next safe week boundary rather than pretending the current week can still move.
  • The site should capture owner intent honestly instead of faking immediate completion.

Response and communication expectations

  • Discord DM can be used for light trade responses and communication nudges.
  • Website acceptance remains the authoritative path for actual trade approval.
  • The trade workflow can record not interested, will consider, and open communication without claiming the trade is accepted.
  • Email is not the preferred trade-communication path in UPS.

Other trade rules

  • Every trade must include at least one non-salary asset.
  • Taxi players acquired in a valid taxi state should land back on taxi automatically.
  • Release 1 supports standard two-team execution only.
  • Three-way or larger trades remain commissioner-managed exceptions until the dedicated workflow is approved.

Example: salary in trade

Team A trades away a player carrying an $80K current-year salary. Team A may include up to $40K of extra salary in the deal. That creates a mirrored cap adjustment: one side gains relief, the other side takes the charge.

Example: started player

If a Sunday 1:00 PM player is included in a trade and that player's game has already kicked off, the trade cannot be processed for that scoring week. If the owners still want the deal, the league should hold it for the next eligible processing window instead of pretending the player can still move now.

Trade-linked extension intent

A trade may include extension intent when the player is extension-eligible and the agreement is clearly stated. The trade page can capture that intent. The final extension validation still belongs to the contract rules, not to casual trade-chat language.

11. League Financing

Cash obligations matter because they tie directly into cap penalties and league accountability. This section should stay boring and unambiguous.

Settled core
League dues $200 Half due by auction Half due by Thanksgiving Late-payment penalty $3K per week

Dues and schedule

  • Annual league dues are $200 per team.
  • Half is due by the start of the free agent auction.
  • The remaining half is due by the Thanksgiving trade deadline.
  • Late payment incurs a $3K cap penalty per week.

Payout structure

  • League Champion: $900
  • Runner-Up: $450
  • 3rd Place: $150
  • Division Winners: $50 each
  • Weekly High Score (Weeks 1-17): $30
  • Hawktuah Bowl Winner: $90

Miscellaneous fees

  • Logo changes cost $15.
  • The MFL hosting fee is part of the league expense structure.

Commissioner remedies

  • Direct a corrective move where the rule already defines a clear obligation.
  • Freeze or hold a workflow while facts are reviewed.
  • Apply cap penalties where the rulebook clearly supports them.
  • Use precedent to resolve silent areas until the written rule catches up.

Special rules and edge cases

  • Jail Bird Rule: legal removal from the league may allow cap-free relief.
  • Retired player rule: retired players can be cut cap-free, though owners may also choose to hold them.
  • Calvin Johnson rule: retirement of a true high-end asset may carry its own compensation logic.

Still needs tightening

14. Example Library

This section consolidates difficult scenarios that owners usually misunderstand on first read. The goal is to make the book usable, not merely formal.

Working draft

Contract example: rookie option

A player drafted at 1.01 in 2025 carries the first-round rookie option path. Before the contract deadline of that player's final rookie season, the owner can exercise the option year instead of letting the rookie contract end and forcing an immediate extension-or-loss choice.

Contract example: MYM

You win a preseason waiver player at $3K and do not file a multi-year deal immediately. Before the Week 2 cutoff, you can use MYM to convert that player into a multi-year contract at the same base salary.

Contract example: extension after trade

You trade for a player in the final year of his contract in midseason. If the league's extension window allows 4 weeks from acquisition, you must handle the extension inside that clock, not months later after the trade has already solved your roster problem.

Trade example: taxi on trade

Team A trades a Round 3 rookie who is still taxi-valid. Team B acquires the player. If Team B has a legal taxi spot and the player still qualifies, UPS should put that player back on taxi automatically rather than forcing a manual dance.

Auction example: missed nominations

During a normal auction day, your franchise owes two nominations. You submit only one and disappear. That is not just bad etiquette. It is a governed auction violation and should enter the missed-nomination penalty ladder.

Roster example: post-kickoff drop

A player is still on your bench, but his 1:00 PM game has started. Even though he never entered your lineup, you still cannot drop him after kickoff.
